Design Process

When I first started looking at my dataset, it became pretty clear to me that I wanted to do a bar chart to show how social media can effect the mental health levels amongst teenagers. My original plan was to create a side-by-side barchart that showed the teen's stress, anxiety, and addiction levels grouped by each social media. The interactive part in this original idea would be a dropdown menu so people could select the age they wanted to look at. Instead of drawing it out on paper, I took to using Tableu to design the bar chart so I could get an exact visualisation that I could aim for when I started coding. However, this barchart felt too messy and complicated to look at, since you had to look at which mental health status and which social media bracket you were looking at, and distinguish the ages you were exploring as well. Though it provided a decent amount of information, it wasn't what I was looking for.

Reasons for Final Graphs

So, after a little trial and error, I decided to split the bar charts up and drop the one focusing on the teen's addiction levels since it wasn't as needed as the other two charts were. With the charts now separated, I decided to make the x-axis based on age and the dropdown menu based on the social media platforms the graphs would be representing. The y-axis is the only one that stayed the same and represents the average stress or anxiety levels of teens who use social media. Though the level scales are both based on the number system 1 through 10, making the average number usually fall between 5 and 6 across the board, it is interesting to see how the numbers fluxuate between the ages depending on if they use TikTok, Instagram, or Both.
As for the physical design of the charts, I wanted the colors to stand out against one another and against the background, so choosing a blue and orange would guarantee a sharp distinction between the two charts even if someone who was colorblind happened to stumble upon the graphs. I also chose to have the graphs be right next to each other instead of stacked ontop of one another so the viewer only had to shift their eyes across the screen to see the different chart instead of having to scroll back and forth to compare them. It also helps that both graphs use the same scale, so it is easy to compare the stress AND anxiety levels of a 14 year old or a 19 year old who use just Instagram.

The Questioning

My initial question when I found this dataset was "What impact does social media have on the mental health levels of teenagers?" This caught my attention as I have noticed how much teenagers have changed over the years with the introduction of social media and constantly having a distraction right in their pockets. From this point, I started breaking the dataset down and found a few key factors that caught my attention: age, platform_usage, stress_level, anxiety_level, and addiction_level. With those in mind, I started asking questions like Which platform had the highest stress levels, which age group felt less anxiety when using Instagram, what's the average addiction level of 15 year olds who use TikTok, etc. I was able to use Tableu to play around with these questions and these visualisations until I landed on my main question: How do the anxiety and stress levels of teenagers of all ages fluxuate with different social media platforms? As my visualisation shows, teenagers who use TikTok without using Instagram have the highest average stress and anxiety levels across all teenage ages. Overall, those who just use Instagram tend to have lower stress and anxiety across all ages as well.
